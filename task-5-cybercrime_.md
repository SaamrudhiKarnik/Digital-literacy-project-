# Part A – Case Study

Case Study: The Spear-Phishing Trap  
What is Phishing?  
Phishing is a form of cybercrime in which attackers pose as genuine individuals or organizations and trick victims into divulging sensitive information such as passwords, bank account information, and personal identification numbers. A variant of phishing is spear-phishing, which is targeted phishing. 

How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)  
Reconnaissance: The attacker identifies the victim, who could be an employee of a company or a student of a university. The attackers use information readily available on social media such as LinkedIn or university websites and social media profiles. 

Crafting the Bait: The attacker sends an email that is apparently from a source the victim trusts. The attackers may pose as the university IT department or a bank. The attackers may also send emails that convey a sense of urgency and may threaten to lock the victim's account if immediate action is not taken. 

The Hook: The attackers send a link to a fake login page that is apparently authentic. The victim believes that the request for a login is genuine and proceeds to click on the link and divulge sensitive information. 

Exploitation: The attackers obtain the login credentials of the victim in real-time and gain access to the victim's email account, bank account, or the company network. 

Who Is Usually Targeted?  
Phishing attacks are not limited to any person. Anyone could be a victim of a phishing attack. Students and university employees are also targeted by attackers. 

Consequences  
The consequences of a phishing attack could be dire. A student of a university lost ₹50,000 from her bank account after she divulged sensitive information such as her bank account and password to an attacker posing as a financial aid department employee of the university. The victim of a phishing attack may suffer not only financial loss but also identity theft and emotional distress.

# Part B – Prevention Checklist:

## **8+ Specific Prevention Tips**

### **1\. Use Strong, Unique Passwords**

Create a different password for each account. Use a combination of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. Avoid using your name, birthdate, or "password123." Consider using a trusted password manager to store them securely.

### **2\. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)**

Turn on 2FA for all critical accounts—email, banking apps, social media, and academic portals. This adds an extra layer of security even if your password is compromised.

### **3\. Verify Links Before Clicking**

Hover over links in emails or messages to preview the actual URL before clicking. Look for spelling errors in domain names (e.g., "[amaz0n.com](https://amaz0n.com/)" instead of "[amazon.com](https://amazon.com/)"). When in doubt, type the official website address directly into your browser.

### **4\. Keep Devices and Apps Updated**

Regularly update your smartphone, laptop, and all applications. These updates often include critical security patches that protect against newly discovered vulnerabilities.

### **5\. Be Skeptical of Unsolicited Calls and Messages**

If you receive a call claiming to be from your bank, a delivery service, or a government agency asking for OTPs, passwords, or personal details, hang up immediately. No legitimate organization will ever ask for sensitive information over the phone.

### **6\. Secure Your Wi-Fi Network**

Change the default password on your home Wi-Fi router to a strong one. Avoid using public Wi-Fi (at cafes, railway stations, airports) for banking or making UPI transactions. If you must use public Wi-Fi, use a trusted VPN.

### **7\. Think Before You Post on Social Media**

Avoid sharing your real-time location, travel plans, or personal details like your student ID, phone number, or home address publicly. Review your privacy settings regularly to control who can see your posts and profile information.

### **8\. Be Cautious with Unknown Links and Attachments**

Do not click on links or download attachments sent via SMS, WhatsApp, or email from unknown senders. These often contain malware or lead to fake login pages designed to steal your credentials.

### **9\. Never Share Your UPI PIN or OTP with Anyone**

Your UPI PIN and OTP (One-Time Password) are the keys to your bank account. No bank, government official, or e-commerce customer support agent will ever ask for these. If someone asks, it is a scam.

### **10\. Use Only Official Apps for UPI Transactions**

Always download UPI apps like Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm, or your bank's app from official app stores (Google Play Store or Apple App Store). Never download payment apps from links sent via SMS or WhatsApp. Enable "UPI PIN for payments" and avoid using "tap and pay" features unless necessary.
